Ashwagandha


Latin name: withania somnifera

Other common name: winter cherry

Family: Solanaceae

Parts used: roots, seeds, leaves


Main Actions

  • Adaptogen, strengthens immune system and helps body cope with many different kinds of stress
  • Traditionally used as a sedative, diuretic, anti-inflammatory, generally for energy, strength, and endurance
  • Modern uses: coughs and colds, ulcers, emaciation, diabetes, epilepsy, insomnia, senile dementia, leprosy,
    Parkinson's disease, cognitive and nervous disorders, rheumatism, arthritis, intestinal infections, bronchitis, asthma,
    sexual enhancement
  • Leaves have analgesic, anti-inflammatory and sedative properties
  • Seeds used for diuretic effects


Cautions/Contraindications

  • High iron content may have abortive effects in large quantities; avoid during pregnancy or use with medical
    supervision.
  • Mild depressant; do not use with alcohol, sedatives, or other antianxiety medications.
  • Use caution if have negative reactions to nightshade plants (potatoes, tomatoes).

Habitat, Growing conditions, Harvesting and Prep

  • Native to India, Africa, the Mediterranean, the Middle East, other locations around the world
  • Likes dry, warm, sunny locations with well drained, sandy, loamy or clay soin with pH 7.5-8 range.
  • Can't tolerate too much water, shade, or cold, especially as seedlings.
  • Start in green house and transplant after one month. Reaches maturity after 5-6 months, when leaves and berries
    dry out.
  • Harvest roots, cut and then dry roots. Can powder or use dry root cuttings.
  • Cultivate like tomatoes.


Historical Uses

  • Used in Ayurvedic, Siddha, and Unari medical traditions in India.
  • Used as general rejuvinating tonic.
